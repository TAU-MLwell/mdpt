The prevalence of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in the United States is approximately 14% among U.S. adults. This translates to about 35.5 million people. This data is based on estimates from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK).

References:
- CDC: [Chronic Kidney Disease in the United States, 2023](https://www.cdc.gov/kidney-disease/php/data-research/index.html)
- NIDDK: [Kidney Disease Statistics for the United States](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/health-statistics/kidney-disease)

The incidence of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in the United States is not typically reported as a percentage of newly diagnosed cases annually, as CKD is a progressive condition that often develops over time. However, the incidence of end-stage renal disease (ESRD), which is the most severe form of CKD, can provide some insight into the progression of CKD.

According to the US Renal Data System 2023 Annual Data Report, there were approximately 130,522 newly registered cases of end-stage renal disease (ESRD) in 2020. This represents a decrease from previous years, indicating a 3.2% decrease from 2019.

References:
- CDC: [Chronic Kidney Disease | Chronic Disease Indicators](https://www.cdc.gov/cdi/indicator-definitions/chronic-kidney-disease.html)
- USRDS: [US Renal Data System 2023 Annual Data Report](https://www.ajkd.org/article/S0272-6386(24)00048-9/fulltext)

The mortality rate for individuals with Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in the United States can be challenging to specify as a single percentage due to variations in stages of CKD and comorbid conditions. However, data from the US Renal Data System (USRDS) provides some insights:

The demographic-adjusted mortality rate among Medicare fee-for-service beneficiaries aged 66 years and older with CKD was 87.9 deaths per 1,000 person-years in 2022. This represents a decline of 21.1% from 112.9 deaths per 1,000 person-years in 2012.

The lifetime risk of developing Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in the United States is estimated to be approximately 59.1% for men and 56.6% for women. This data is derived from a study that used a Markov Monte Carlo model to simulate the lifetime risk of CKD stages 3-5.

References:

The survival rate for individuals with Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in the United States varies significantly depending on the stage of the disease and other health factors. However, specific survival rates for CKD as a whole are not typically reported in a single percentage due to the progressive nature of the disease and the influence of comorbid conditions.

For more detailed survival statistics, the US Renal Data System (USRDS) provides data on outcomes for patients with end-stage renal disease (ESRD), which is the most severe form of CKD. According to the USRDS 2023 Annual Data Report, the survival rates for patients with ESRD have improved over the years due to advancements in treatment and care.

Unfortunately, a specific overall survival rate percentage for all stages of CKD is not readily available from the provided sources.
